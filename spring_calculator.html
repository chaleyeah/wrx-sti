Notes
Wheel rate = spring rate × MR². Motion ratio here is defined as spring travel ÷ wheel travel (a number < 1 because the spring is inboard of the contact patch and moves less than the wheel). Because it's squared, a 0.90 MR drops effective rate to 81% of the spring's nominal rate — the single biggest reason a stiff-looking spring feels softer at the tire.
Motion ratio defaults are estimates. Front ≈ 0.95 (near-coaxial strut, mostly the strut-angle cosine) and rear ≈ 0.72 (double-wishbone with the spring mounted inboard) are reasonable starting points for the 2015–2021 VA chassis, but they are not measured factory figures. For real tuning, measure your own: jack the wheel through its travel and compare spring compression to wheel movement, or use the geometry helper with arm/mount distances from your car.
Ride frequency = (1 / 2π) × √(wheel rate / sprung corner mass). This is what suspension tuners actually target. Rough guideline: 1.0–1.5 Hz street comfort, 1.5–2.0 Hz sport/street, 2.0–2.5 Hz autocross/track, 2.5 Hz+ aero/slick race cars. A balanced setup usually runs the rear ~0.1–0.3 Hz higher than the front so the rear settles first and the car feels planted (flat ride).
Sprung corner mass = (total weight × distribution ÷ 2) − unsprung corner mass. Default 3,550 lb assumes ~3,400 lb curb plus driver; 59% front is typical for the EJ-powered STI's nose-heavy layout. Adjust to your corner-balanced scale numbers for accuracy.
This ignores the anti-roll bars (which add roll-only wheel rate), tire spring rate (in series with the suspension, lowers effective rate slightly at high frequency), and any rising/falling-rate geometry through travel. It's a static, single-corner estimate for comparing spring choices — not a full kinematics model.
